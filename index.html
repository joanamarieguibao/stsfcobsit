Promoting Indigenous Products Using Facebook Pages

A digital approach to empowering local heritage and entrepreneurship

About

Facebook is one of the most popular social media platforms today. It provides a space for individuals and communities to promote their products easily.

Indigenous products such as hand-crafted items, traditional handwoven textiles, locally sourced food, and artisanal goods are not just commodities to purchase. But they represent cultural expression, tradition, and creativity. Products each entail cultural storytelling, represented from practices unique to the indigenous community, capturing the heritage/skill as well as the value of the community.

Indigenous artisans create Facebook pages to relate to consumers and promote their goods inexpensively while sharing their stories. It provides cultural heritage preservation and livelihood creation. By enhancing accessibility to Indigenous crafted goods, it encourages a greater appreciation of artisanship and cultural preservation in the 21st century.

Historical Content

Indigenous communities in the Philippines used to barter locally for handmade, artisan goods like woven textiles, beadwork and pottery. These crafts were a way to remember their cultural practices, rituals, and essential forms of cultural identity.

While colonization introduced foreign products, Indigenous crafts retained meaning as communities practiced and saved their handmade talents. Later in the 20th century, governments and cultural festivals promoted the revival of Indigenous industries and crafts.

Currently, Facebook Pages facilitate the promotion of Indigenous products, allowing artisans to reach national and global markets with "photos" of their products, "video", and stories of those who invested handmade arts and crafts with their practices and rituals, and through their local communities. The resurgence of Indigenous industries and products is reflective of the convergence between tradition and technology that is rediscovering Indigenous ways of knowing and maintenance of culture and livelihoods through social media.

Contemporary Relevance

Storytelling

With the use of Facebook, the origin stories behind products from various Indigenous tribes are spread worldwide, preserving their culture in the midst of modernization.

Sustainability

Most Indigenous products use environment-friendly materials, therefore promoting these products means promoting sustainability.

E-Commerce

Through Facebook, Indigenous products are promoted around the world, making it accessible even in the international market, increasing sales and demand for the products.

Then

Before Digital Platforms

- Merchants promoted their products mainly through local markets, relying on positive customer experiences to build trust and attract buyers from nearby towns.

- Cultural preservation was maintained through traditional practices and storytelling.

Shared Values Across Time

- A deep commitment to preserving culture and craftsmanship remains central.

- Whether offline or online, Indigenous entrepreneurs continue to promote their culture and creativity.

Now

With Facebook and Digital Platforms

- Facebook Pages allow artisans to promote products globally, connecting directly with customers.

- Storytelling has evolved into digital narratives through photos, videos, and posts that promote Indigenous culture.

Success Stories of Indigenous S&T Integration

Case Study 1:

Abra Indigo – Hand-woven Products by the Itneg Community (Abra, Philippines)

Abra Indigo is a social enterprise in partnership with the Itneg weavers of Abra and founded by Ysabelle Sarayba. The Abra-based company handcrafts fabrics, fashion accessories, and home décor. During the pandemic, this brand used Facebook Pages among other social media to sell unique face masks that Itneg had woven from excess fabric.

Abra Indigo spoke of the rich culture and crafts of the Itneg through engaging storytelling and visually appealing posts, thereby creating sustainable income for the weavers. In this way, it could reach out to customers from across the country and raise further awareness of how to support indigenous artisans.

Key Learnings:

Each weave had some story associated with it, and that was elaborated through Facebook; it connected buyers emotionally to the weave. Social media has made indigenous products visible beyond the local market. It underlined the authenticity and rootedness in culture, which served to increase the level of trust and appreciation for local craftsmanship.

Case Study 2:

Four H Handicraft – Empowering Ifugao Women through Handicrafts (Nueva Vizcaya, Philippines)

The women-managed Four H Handicraft based in Nueva Vizcaya trains and employs women from Ifugao who manufacture handicrafts made of indigenous materials friendly to the environment. It has put up on its Facebook pages targeted for export customers and buyers abroad handicraft kitchenware, baskets and furniture accessories. It is the online visibility which has allowed them to create revenue streams even for rural women, mothers, and elderly by preserving the skills of traditional craftsmanship.

Key Learnings:

It helps the rural artisans reach out to both the urban and international markets. Social media presence fuels community pride and business credibility.

Case Study 3:

Balay ni Atong - The Online Preservation of the Ilocano Weaving Heritage (Vigan, Ilocos Sur, Philippines)

Atong Francisco, the weaver and cultural advocate, created the social enterprise Balay ni Atong, which works with the Ilocano artisans to restore the Abel Iloco weaving tradition. The brand's Facebook page features weavers' lives, their cultural background, and the long and painstaking processes of creating textiles by hand.

In addition to sharing very attractive product photos, that pandemic started composing weaving tutorials and facts about Abel cloth—Facebook became their main marketing and sales channel and even contacted clients in Metro Manila and other countries.

Key Learnings:

Storytelling through social media helps the younger generation to know about the intangible heritage. It has been a very successful cultural branding and customer engagement tool. As a result, being online means a direct contribution to the rural people's income and the continuation of eco-friendly textile production.

Recommendations

Digital Literacy Training

Organize workshops for indigenous communities on how to manage Facebook pages, create engaging content, and respond to customers professionally.

Youth Involvement

Engage the younger members of indigenous communities in managing digital platforms, integrating traditional knowledge with modern technology.

Cultural Sensitivity and Authenticity

Ensure that online marketing respects cultural values and avoids exploiting traditional designs or practices.
